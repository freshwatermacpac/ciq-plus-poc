The story in brief

At the 2025 election, Labor promised not to touch negative gearing or the capital gains tax discount. A year later it legislated changes to both. This is the story of how that happened — the Treasury Laws Amendment (Tax Reform No. 1) Bill 2026 and its companion, the Income Tax Rates Amendment (Tax Reform No. 1) Bill 2026.

Re-elected, Treasurer Jim Chalmers reopened the tax debate through an Economic Reform Roundtable in mid-2025, and a Senate inquiry into the CGT discount from November built the evidence base. Hearings in February 2026 drew Ken Henry, the OECD, the ACTU and the property lobby into a sharp public fight over how Australia taxes investment income.

The 2026–27 Budget, handed down on 12 May 2026, set the package: a permanent $250 Working Australians Tax Offset and a $1,000 instant tax deduction for workers; negative gearing limited to new builds for properties bought after Budget night; and the 50% CGT discount replaced with cost base indexation plus a 30% minimum tax, so only real gains are taxed. The Bills were introduced on 29 May as the “first tranche” of reform.

What followed was a six-week brawl: business, property and farm peaks against; ACOSS, unions and welfare groups for. The Government added small-business carve-outs and struck a deal with the Greens, and the package passed Parliament in late June 2026. This brief lets you follow that whole arc, and read every primary source behind it.
