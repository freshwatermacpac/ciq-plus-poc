The story in brief

Australia spent 2025 rebuilding the legal foundation of its aged care system. A new, rights-based Aged Care Act — the central response to the Royal Commission — was due to commence on 1 July, alongside a new in-home care program, Support at Home, replacing Home Care Packages and the Commonwealth Home Support Programme.

It didn't go to plan. As detail landed late, providers led by Ageing Australia warned in April that the July switch was "impossible." After the May federal election and a change of minister — from Anika Wells to Sam Rae — the government deferred commencement to 1 November. Advocates were split: some welcomed breathing room, others feared older people would keep paying under the old rules for longer.

The second half of the year was a sprint to be ready: a levy bill and Senate inquiry, the final Aged Care Rules in late September, a first Complaints Commissioner, and a push to release 20,000 new Home Care Packages before the deadline. The Act commenced on 1 November.

By December the question had shifted from "will it start?" to "is it working?" — with COTA, NACA and the Opposition all pressing on home-care delivery, fees and co-payments. This brief lets you follow that whole arc, and read every primary source behind it.
